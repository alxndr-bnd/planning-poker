What Is Planning Poker? A Complete Guide to Scrum Estimation

Planning Poker, also called Scrum Poker, is a consensus-based agile estimation technique that helps software teams size the work in their backlog. Instead of one person guessing how long a task will take, every member of the team estimates at the same time using numbered cards. The goal is not a perfect number; it is a shared understanding of the work and an honest conversation about what each item really involves.

The method was popularized by James Grenning in 2002 and later spread widely through Mike Cohn's writing on agile estimating. Today it is one of the most common ways to assign story points during sprint planning and backlog refinement. Why Teams Use Planning Poker

Estimating is hard, and people are influenced by whoever speaks first. Planning Poker is designed to counter that. Because everyone reveals their card simultaneously, it reduces anchoring, the bias where the first number mentioned pulls all later estimates toward it. The benefits teams report most often are:

  • Better conversations: a wide spread of estimates surfaces hidden assumptions, missing acceptance criteria, and risks before coding begins.
  • Shared ownership: the whole team commits to an estimate, not just a lead or a manager.
  • Relative sizing: comparing items to each other is faster and more reliable than predicting exact hours.
  • Engagement: the simple act of playing a card keeps quieter members involved and gives everyone an equal voice.

How to Play Planning Poker, Step by Step

A single round of agile estimation is quick once the team has the rhythm. Here is the standard flow:

  • 1. Present the item. The Product Owner reads a backlog item or user story and clarifies the goal and acceptance criteria.
  • 2. Discuss briefly. The team asks questions to understand scope. This is for clarification, not for proposing numbers yet.
  • 3. Estimate privately. Each member selects a card representing their estimate in story points, keeping it hidden.
  • 4. Reveal together. Everyone shows their card at the same moment so no one is influenced by others.
  • 5. Discuss the spread. If estimates differ widely, the highest and lowest voters explain their reasoning.
  • 6. Re-vote. Estimate again after the discussion, repeating until the team reaches consensus.
  • 7. Record and move on. Note the agreed estimate and pick up the next item.

The Fibonacci Scale and Why the Gaps Grow

Most teams estimate with a modified Fibonacci sequence: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20, 40, 100, plus a few special cards. The growing gaps are the whole point. The larger a task is, the less precisely anyone can size it, so it makes no sense to offer a 14 next to a 13. The widening intervals force a choice between roughly understood buckets rather than false precision.

Common non-numeric cards include:

  • 0 for work that is already done or trivial.
  • ? (question mark) for "I don't understand this well enough to estimate."
  • Coffee cup to request a break.
  • Infinity for something far too large to estimate as-is.

A high estimate such as 20 or 40 is usually a signal to split the item into smaller stories before it enters a sprint. You can find short definitions of these terms in our glossary.

Story Points vs Hours

Story points measure the relative effort, complexity, and uncertainty of a piece of work rather than the clock time it takes. A two-point task is meant to feel roughly twice the size of a one-point task. Teams favor points over hours for several reasons: people are far better at comparing sizes than predicting exact durations, points stay stable even when a faster or slower person picks up the work, and they avoid the false confidence that an "8-hour" estimate creates. Over a few sprints a team learns its velocity, the number of points it typically completes, which makes forecasting far more dependable than summing guessed hours.

When to Re-Vote and Discuss High-Low Splits

Differences are not a problem to suppress; they are the most valuable output of the session. When the cards reveal a split, ask the person with the highest estimate and the person with the lowest to explain their thinking. Often the high voter knows about an edge case or dependency the others missed, or the low voter knows a shortcut. After a short discussion the team re-votes. Repeat until estimates converge, but do not chase perfect agreement: once the team is within one or two adjacent cards, take the higher value or the rough majority and move on. Endless re-voting wastes the time the technique is meant to save.

Roles in a Planning Poker Session

  • Scrum Master (or facilitator): runs the session, keeps it time-boxed, ensures cards are revealed simultaneously, and prevents one voice from dominating. The Scrum Master does not usually estimate.
  • Product Owner: presents each item, answers scope and priority questions, and clarifies acceptance criteria. The Product Owner answers but does not vote, since they do not do the implementation.
  • Development team: the engineers, testers, and designers who will actually build the work. They are the ones who play cards and own the estimates.
  • Observers: stakeholders or guests may watch to build context, but they stay silent during voting so they do not bias the team.

Live vs Async Estimation

Planning Poker works in two modes. Live sessions, whether in a room or on a video call, keep the energy high and let discussion flow naturally; everyone votes in the same few minutes. Async estimation suits distributed teams across time zones: members cast votes on backlog items when it suits them, and the facilitator follows up on big disagreements later. Async trades the instant back-and-forth for flexibility, so it works best when stories are already well written. For remote-specific tips, see our guide to Planning Poker for remote teams.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Anchoring out loud: never let someone announce a number before cards are revealed. It defeats the purpose.
  • Treating points as hours: converting points back into a fixed number of hours throws away their main advantage.
  • Estimating unclear stories: if half the team plays a question mark, refine the story first.
  • Over-discussing: time-box each item; a few minutes of debate is healthy, twenty is not.
  • Comparing velocity between teams: points are relative to one team's scale and mean nothing across teams.
  • Letting managers vote: only the people doing the work should estimate it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Planning Poker the same as Scrum Poker?
Yes. The two names refer to the same consensus-based estimation technique. "Scrum Poker" simply emphasizes its common use within the Scrum framework.
How long should a Planning Poker session take?
Aim for one to two minutes per item once the team is warmed up. Backlog refinement sessions are often time-boxed to an hour so the team stays focused.
What if the team cannot reach consensus?
Discuss the high and low estimates, then re-vote. If the spread persists, the story is probably unclear or too large, so split it or gather more information rather than forcing a number.

Can remote teams use Planning Poker?
