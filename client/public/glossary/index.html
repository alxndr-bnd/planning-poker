Agile Estimation Glossary

A plain-language reference to the terms you will hear during Planning Poker and agile estimation sessions. New to the practice? Start with our deep guide on what Planning Poker is, then run a real session in our free, open-source Planning Poker tool.

Planning Poker
Planning Poker is a consensus-based, gamified technique for estimating the effort of work items. Each participant privately selects a card representing their estimate, and all cards are revealed at once to avoid influencing one another. When estimates diverge, the team discusses the reasons and votes again until they converge. You can try it live in our free Planning Poker tool.
Scrum Poker
Scrum Poker is another common name for Planning Poker, emphasizing its frequent use by Scrum teams during Sprint Planning and backlog refinement. The mechanics are identical: private voting followed by a simultaneous reveal. The term is used interchangeably with Planning Poker across most agile literature.
Story Points
Story points are a unit-less measure of the relative effort, complexity, and uncertainty involved in completing a work item. Instead of estimating in hours, teams compare items against one another so a 5-point story is roughly five times the effort of a 1-point story. Because points are relative, they stay meaningful even as a team's pace changes over time. See our guide on how story points work in Planning Poker.
Velocity
Velocity is the number of story points a team completes in a single sprint, averaged over several sprints to smooth out variation. It is used for forecasting how much work the team can realistically take on in upcoming sprints. Velocity is a team-specific metric and should never be compared between teams, since their point scales differ. It is a planning aid, not a performance target.
Fibonacci Sequence (in Estimation)
The Fibonacci sequence (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, ...) is the most popular scale for Planning Poker cards. Its widening gaps reflect the reality that larger items carry more uncertainty, so the difference between an 8 and a 13 should be coarser than between a 1 and a 2. The growing spread discourages false precision and forces teams to break very large items down before estimating them.
T-shirt Sizing
T-shirt sizing is a lightweight estimation scale using labels like XS, S, M, L, and XL instead of numbers. It is often used early in planning, for high-level roadmap items, or with stakeholders who find numeric points abstract. Sizes can later be mapped to story points once the work is broken down further. It trades precision for speed and shared intuition.
Anchoring Bias
Anchoring bias is the tendency to rely too heavily on the first number you hear when forming an estimate. If one person says a story is "an 8" out loud before voting, others unconsciously drift toward that figure. Planning Poker counters anchoring by keeping each vote private until everyone reveals simultaneously, preserving independent judgment.
Consensus Estimation
Consensus estimation is the practice of reaching a shared estimate through discussion rather than averaging or having one person decide. Outliers explain their reasoning, the team surfaces hidden assumptions, and the group converges on a number everyone can support. This shared understanding is often more valuable than the number itself, because it aligns the team on scope and risk.
Estimation (Relative vs Absolute)
Absolute estimation assigns a concrete figure such as "this will take 12 hours," while relative estimation compares items to each other without committing to time. Agile teams favor relative estimation because people are far better at judging that one task is bigger than another than at predicting exact durations. Story points and T-shirt sizes are both relative scales.
Sprint Planning
Sprint Planning is the Scrum event at the start of each sprint where the team decides what to deliver and how. The Product Owner presents prioritized backlog items, the team estimates and clarifies them, and together they commit to a sprint goal. Planning Poker is frequently used during this meeting to size the candidate work.
Product Backlog
The Product Backlog is the single, ordered list of everything that might be needed in the product, owned and prioritized by the Product Owner. Items near the top are refined and estimated in detail, while lower items remain coarse until they rise in priority. It is a living document that evolves continuously as the product and market change.
User Story
A user story is a short, plain-language description of a feature told from the perspective of the person who wants it, often in the form "As a [user], I want [goal] so that [benefit]." Stories keep the focus on user value rather than technical tasks and are the typical unit estimated in Planning Poker. Good stories are small, testable, and independently deliverable.
Observer / Spectator
An observer, or spectator, is a session participant who watches the estimation without casting a vote. Stakeholders, managers, or new team members often join in this role to follow the discussion without skewing the outcome. Our Planning Poker tool supports a dedicated spectator mode so guests can join without affecting the result.
Reveal
The reveal is the moment when all hidden votes are turned face up at the same time. Simultaneous revealing is the core mechanic that protects estimates from anchoring bias, since no one can adjust after seeing another's card. A wide spread of revealed values signals that the team should discuss before agreeing.
Re-vote
A re-vote is a fresh round of voting held after the team discusses a divergent result. Participants who held extreme values explain their reasoning, then everyone votes again with the new context. Rounds repeat until the estimates converge closely enough to call a consensus.
Scrum Master
The Scrum Master is the servant-leader who helps the team apply Scrum effectively, removes impediments, and facilitates events such as Sprint Planning. During Planning Poker they often run the session, keep discussion productive, and protect the process from anchoring or pressure. They do not assign estimates themselves.
Product Owner
The Product Owner is accountable for maximizing the value of the product and manages the Product Backlog. They define and prioritize items, clarify requirements during estimation, and decide what the team works on next. While they answer scope questions during Planning Poker, the estimates themselves come from the people doing the work.
Definition of Ready
The Definition of Ready is a shared checklist of conditions a backlog item must meet before the team is willing to estimate or commit to it. Typical criteria include a clear description, acceptance criteria, and no major blocking dependencies. It prevents the team from wasting a Planning Poker round on items that are still too vague to size.
Definition of Done
The Definition of Done is the team's shared agreement on what it means for a work item to be truly complete, covering aspects like testing, code review, and documentation. It creates a consistent quality bar so "done" means the same thing to everyone. A clear Definition of Done makes estimates more reliable, because everyone is sizing the same scope of work.
Backlog Refinement
Backlog refinement, sometimes called grooming, is the ongoing activity of adding detail, estimates, and order to Product Backlog items. It keeps upcoming work ready for Sprint Planning so meetings stay focused and efficient. Planning Poker is commonly used in refinement sessions to size items as they are clarified.
Three-Point Estimation
Three-point estimation combines an optimistic, a most likely, and a pessimistic estimate to account for uncertainty in a single figure. It is more often used in traditional project management, but the underlying idea of reasoning about a range rather than one number complements agile estimation well. Planning Poker captures a similar instinct through its discussion of outliers.
